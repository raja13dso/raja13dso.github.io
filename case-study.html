01

THE PROBLEM
Setting the stage

Evernorth Health Services, the health services segment of the Cigna Group, operates at the intersection of healthcare, pharmacy, and enterprise benefits — one of the most regulated and risk-sensitive environments in corporate America. When I stepped into the BISO role, I was embedded directly into the HR and Legal IT business units: two divisions that sit at the apex of employee data privacy, legal privilege, and workforce compliance data.

The mandate was clear but the problem was layered. Security at this scale is not a technology problem — it is a trust and governance problem. Engineering teams were building faster than security posture could track. AI and LLM capabilities were being adopted across business functions without established risk frameworks. Regulatory exposure under HIPAA, PCI-DSS, and state-level data privacy laws was real and growing.

The fundamental question was: how do you operationalise meaningful security risk reduction across business units that have complex, competing priorities — without becoming the team that slows everything down?

Security at enterprise scale is not a technology problem. It is a trust and governance problem — won or lost in the relationships between security advisors and the business units they serve.

Approach principle — BISO engagement model
HR & Legal IT — Unique Risk Profile

These teams handle the most sensitive non-clinical data Cigna produces: workforce PII, legal correspondence, employment records, and attorney-client privileged communications. A breach here carries regulatory, reputational, and legal consequence simultaneously.

The AI Inflection Point

In 2025, AI adoption across enterprise functions accelerated sharply. LLMs were being integrated into HR workflows, legal document drafting, and compliance monitoring — often faster than security governance could formally assess each use case.

02

RESEARCH PHASE
Understanding before acting

The first weeks in a BISO role are diagnostic. Before proposing solutions, I needed to understand the actual security posture — not the documented posture, but the lived, operational reality of each business unit. This required a structured discovery process across four dimensions.

Discovery Dimensions
WEEK 1–2
Stakeholder Mapping & Trust Building

Conducted structured interviews with HR IT leads, Legal IT directors, and application owners to understand their workflows, pain points, and existing risk perception. The goal was not to audit — it was to listen. Security teams that lead with audits lose the room immediately.

WEEK 2–4
Application Security Landscape Assessment

Reviewed application portfolios across both business units — cataloguing data flows, third-party integrations, API surface areas, and CI/CD pipeline maturity. Cross-referenced findings against Cigna security policies and industry baselines (OWASP ASVS Level 2, NIST CSF).

WEEK 3–5
AI/LLM Usage Inventory

Worked with business unit leads to surface all AI tool usage — both sanctioned and shadow. This included LLM-based document drafting tools, HR co-pilots, and embedded analytics. Assessed each against NIST AI RMF principles: govern, map, measure, and manage.

WEEK 4–6
KRI Baseline & Compliance Gap Analysis

Established a baseline for key risk indicators — open vulnerabilities by severity, patching SLA adherence, training completion rates, and audit finding recurrence. Mapped current posture against PCI-DSS, HIPAA, and Cigna's internal security standards to surface gaps requiring immediate prioritisation.

The research phase confirmed a consistent pattern: security risks were not hidden — they were visible to the right people, but not communicated upward in a language that business leaders could act on. The measurement problem was as significant as the risk problem itself.

Key insight — discovery phase
03

STRATEGIC THINKING
How I shaped the approach

The BISO model is fundamentally different from a traditional security architect role. Rather than designing technical controls, the BISO operates as a risk translator — converting technical security realities into business risk language that stakeholders can understand, prioritise, and act on.

My strategic approach rested on three principles that I brought from prior experience at Goldman Sachs and Oracle, adapted to Cigna's unique healthcare and regulatory context.

Three Strategic Principles
01
Business-First Risk Language

Every risk finding was framed in terms of business impact — regulatory exposure, reputational risk, operational disruption — not just CVE severity scores or technical debt.

02
Security as an Enabler

Positioned security review as a service that accelerates safe adoption of new capabilities (especially AI), rather than a gate that blocks progress. This required changing the conversation from "you can't do this" to "here's how we do this safely."

03
Visibility Creates Accountability

Real-time KRI dashboards ensure that risk posture is not a quarterly audit exercise — it is a live operational metric that business leaders check alongside their other KPIs.

For the AI/LLM risk dimension specifically, I drew on my prior work implementing LLM guardrails at Goldman Sachs to develop a governance framework tailored to healthcare's unique requirements. Healthcare data processed through AI systems carries HIPAA implications that financial services AI governance does not — the approach had to be adapted, not simply copied.

I aligned the AI risk framework to NIST AI RMF's four functions: Govern (establishing policies and accountability), Map (identifying AI use cases and their risk context), Measure (evaluating risk indicators), and Manage (implementing controls and monitoring). This provided a structured, auditable approach that satisfied both security and compliance stakeholders.

Regulatory Compliance Architecture

The security programme was designed to satisfy overlapping regulatory requirements simultaneously — reducing compliance overhead through shared control architectures that address PCI-DSS, HIPAA, and NIST requirements with single implementations wherever possible.

04

CHALLENGES &
Solutions paired

No strategic security programme unfolds without friction. The challenges encountered at Cigna were not unique to one organisation — they reflect the structural tensions inherent to enterprise security governance in large regulated businesses. What matters is how they were addressed.

⚡ Challenge 01
AI Tool Shadow Adoption

Business units were adopting AI-powered productivity tools — document drafting, data analysis co-pilots, HR query assistants — outside of formal IT procurement. These tools were processing sensitive employee and legal data without security review or data residency validation.

✓ Solution Applied
AI Inventory + Risk-Tiered Approval Fast Track

Conducted a structured AI usage inventory across both business units. Developed a risk-tiered approval framework that categorised AI tools by data sensitivity and processing scope — allowing low-risk tools to receive expedited approval, while higher-risk integrations underwent full security review. This eliminated "shadow AI" without creating friction that would drive further shadow usage.

⚡ Challenge 02
Risk Communication Gap

Security findings were being captured in technical vulnerability reports that business stakeholders could not meaningfully act on. The result was a disconnect: security teams knew what the risks were; business leaders could not prioritise them against operational demands.

✓ Solution Applied
KRI Dashboard with Business-Linked Risk Narratives

Redesigned risk reporting to pair KRI metrics with business-impact narratives. Instead of "14 high-severity CVEs open," the dashboard showed "3 risks with regulatory exposure under HIPAA §164.306 — estimated remediation window: 18 days." Stakeholders could immediately understand the priority and business consequence without needing technical security expertise.

⚡ Challenge 03
Secure SDLC Inconsistency

Development teams within HR IT and Legal IT were operating at different levels of SDLC maturity. Some had adopted basic CI/CD scanning; others had minimal security testing integration. This inconsistency created uneven risk exposure across the application portfolio.

✓ Solution Applied
Secure SDLC Modernisation Programme

Designed and launched a phased Secure SDLC modernisation programme — establishing baseline security requirements for all teams, implementing security checkpoints at design, build, and release stages, and embedding AppSec champions within development squads. The programme was tailored to each team's existing maturity to avoid imposing controls that were impractical given their current tooling and processes.

⚡ Challenge 04
LLM-Specific Risk in Legal Context

Legal IT teams were the highest-risk environment for LLM adoption: attorney-client privilege, discovery obligations, and data confidentiality requirements created a uniquely complex risk profile. Standard AI governance frameworks did not adequately address legal-domain-specific requirements.

✓ Solution Applied
Legal-Domain AI Risk Framework

Collaborated with Legal IT leadership and the Legal department to develop a bespoke AI risk framework for legal workflows — covering data residency, output privilege classification, audit trail requirements, and hallucination risk in legal document contexts. This framework was aligned to NIST AI RMF Govern and Measure functions and reviewed against Cigna's legal and compliance standards.

05

EXECUTION
What was built and delivered

Strategic clarity is only as valuable as the execution that follows it. Across the first months in role, several concrete programmes were initiated or accelerated. Each was designed to deliver measurable risk reduction while building the capabilities that would sustain improvement beyond any individual intervention.

Delivery Workstreams
STREAM 01
KRI Dashboard Design & Deployment

Designed real-time KRI dashboards for application security posture across HR IT and Legal IT. Metrics included vulnerability ageing by severity, SDLC gate compliance rates, security training completion, and AI tool risk classification status. Dashboards were made accessible to both security teams and non-technical business leaders — with separate views calibrated to each audience's decision-making needs.

STREAM 02
Secure SDLC Programme Modernisation

Led the modernisation of Secure SDLC practices across development teams — introducing security requirements at sprint planning, design review gates for high-risk features, and automated scanning integrated into existing CI/CD tooling. AppSec champion roles were established within development squads to create an internal security capability that persists beyond direct advisory involvement.

STREAM 03
AI/LLM Risk Governance Programme

Built and deployed an AI risk governance programme encompassing: a risk-tiered approval workflow for new AI tools, an ongoing AI inventory and classification process, technical guardrails aligned to NIST AI RMF, and monitoring procedures for AI systems handling sensitive data. Collaborated with Cigna's enterprise AI governance team to ensure alignment with the organisation-wide AI strategy.

STREAM 04
Stakeholder Risk Engagement Cadence

Established regular security risk reviews with HR IT and Legal IT leadership — monthly KRI reviews, quarterly strategic risk assessments, and ad-hoc security consultations for new product initiatives or technology adoptions. This cadence converted security from a reactive audit function into a proactive business partner relationship.

STREAM 05
Regulatory Compliance Alignment

Coordinated compliance posture reviews against PCI-DSS v4.0, HIPAA Security Rule, and NIST AI RMF across both business units. Identified gaps between current controls and required standards, prioritised remediation by risk exposure, and tracked remediation progress through the KRI dashboard system.

07

FRAMEWORKS
& Tools applied

The technical and governance frameworks deployed in this engagement were selected for their fit to the specific risk context of a large regulated healthcare organisation — not applied generically. Each framework was adapted to Cigna's operating environment, existing security standards, and the maturity level of the teams involved.

Security Governance
  • NIST CSF 2.0 — Organisational security programme structure and maturity benchmarking
  • NIST 800-53 — Control selection and implementation guidance for HR/Legal IT systems
  • OWASP ASVS Level 2 — Application security verification baseline for in-scope applications
  • Cigna Security Standards — Internal policy alignment and control inheritance
Regulatory Compliance
  • HIPAA Security Rule — Administrative, physical, and technical safeguards for ePHI-adjacent systems
  • PCI-DSS v4.0 — Payment data protection controls for applicable application scope
  • State Privacy Laws — CCPA and applicable state-level data privacy requirements
  • NIST AI RMF — Govern, Map, Measure, Manage functions for AI/LLM risk
AI Security Methodology

For AI and LLM risk specifically, I developed a four-stage evaluation methodology drawing on NIST AI RMF and my prior experience implementing LLM guardrails at Goldman Sachs:

G
Govern

Establish policies, roles, and accountability structures for AI use across business units.

M
Map

Inventory AI tools, classify by data sensitivity, and identify risk context for each use case.

M
Measure

Define and track risk indicators — data exposure, output reliability, audit trail completeness.

M
Manage

Implement controls, monitor continuously, and update the risk profile as AI capabilities evolve.

08

REFLECTION
What this role demands

The BISO role is one of the most demanding positions in enterprise security — not because of its technical complexity, but because of its relational complexity. Success is not measured in firewall rules or CVE counts. It is measured in the quality of conversations you enable between security and the business, and in the security decisions that business leaders make well because they were better informed.

Three lessons stand out from this engagement so far.

Lesson 01
Speed of Trust is the Security Control

The faster a business unit leader trusts the security advisor's judgment, the faster risk findings move to remediation. Trust-building is not soft work — it is the highest-leverage security investment in a BISO role. Every interaction is either building or eroding that trust.

Lesson 02
AI Risk is a Moving Target — Governance Must Move Too

AI capabilities deployed in 2025 will have different risk profiles in 2026. Static governance frameworks become liabilities. The AI risk programme must be designed to update continuously — with clear triggers for re-evaluation when new capabilities are adopted or when the threat landscape changes.

Lesson 03
Measurement is the Missing Ingredient in Most Security Programmes

Most organisations know they have security risk. Few have the visibility to prioritise it effectively. The KRI dashboard work at Cigna confirmed a pattern I first observed at Goldman Sachs: when business leaders can see risk in real time, they make better security decisions — without needing to be security experts themselves. Measurement creates accountability that no policy can.

The future of enterprise security is not a more powerful scanning tool or a more comprehensive policy document. It is a security function so embedded in the business that engineering leaders ask "how do we do this securely?" before they ask "can we do this at all?"

Raja Kumar Sharma — on the evolution of enterprise security leadership
